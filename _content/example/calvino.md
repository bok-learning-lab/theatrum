## The course

A Comparative Literature seminar on Italo Calvino, built around *Six Memos for the Next Millennium*, the three late novels (*The Castle of Crossed Destinies*, *Invisible Cities*, *If on a winter's night a traveler*), and a frame of cybernetics, information theory, semiotics, and Oulipian constraint.

What the course wants any final assessment to carry:

- command of Calvino's corpus and its place in 20th-century modernism
- fluency in Oulipo, cybernetics, structuralism, information theory
- working understanding of combinatorics and constraint as compositional method
- the ability to read Calvino's prose at a fine grain — voice, cadence, image, the recursive turn of a sentence
- the ability to take a position about literature, computation, and the place of the reader, and defend it

## The essay version

A ten-plus-page final paper. The student picks two or three readings, exercises one or two interpretive mechanics — compare, synthesize, argue — builds a thesis, lands a conclusion.

The scaffolded essay has been at the center of literary education for about as long (a version of the required first-year writing course at Harvard is roughly 150 years old). Why? The essay carried three professional functions on a single artifact: the best vehicle most departments had for *teaching* students how to think within a discipline, the best instrument they had for *assessing* whether the student had learned, and a close match to the form of academic *communication* the professor was already producing in their own research. Student learning, faculty assessment, and disciplinary publication all rode on the same object. That alignment is what made the essay the "apex predator" of the teaching-and-learning ecosystem for so long: not that it was uniquely good at any one of those three things, but that it did all three at once.

The condition that held the alignment together is no longer entirely intact. When the artifact a student turns in can be produced by a tool the student has access to and the instructor cannot reliably detect, the inferential link between the artifact and the student's learning weakens. Teaching and disciplinary communication still ride on the essay more or less as they always did. The assessment function is the one that has come loose. Faculty are now in the position of needing to decompose, more explicitly than they have had to in a century and a half, what the essay was tacitly carrying — and to rebuild those functions across new instruments.

## The system version

Calvino died before delivering the sixth of the *Six Memos for the Next Millennium*. Five were completed — lightness, quickness, exactitude, visibility, multiplicity. The sixth, on *consistency*, was never written.

**The system assignment, the course's capstone, is to do a forgery of it.**

Not an essay *about* what Calvino might have said. The missing memo itself: in his voice, his cadence, his images, his recursions — a plausible sixth chapter of the book. And not by typing it, but by building a system: 

1) a curated corpus of texts as input
2) a chain of operations that decompose the act of writing the memo into its constituent moves
2) the final output: the student's Sixth Memo and a process document outlining *the how* and *the why* of its creation 

The deliverable is the system, the memo it produces, and a defense of both.

## What the students actually have to do

### 01 — assemble the corpus

This is up to the student. It could be Calvino's other five memos. The 1967 lecture on *Cybernetics and Ghosts*. The relevant Oulipo material. Possibly the journalism, the letters, the introductions. Possibly secondary criticism. *Possibly not*. The student makes those calls and defends them.

A traditional lit review asks the student to find sources they will *cite*. This asks them to find sources who will be structural to their system. 

### 02 — decompose the act of writing

A single prompt that says "write the sixth memo in Calvino's voice" produces slop. The student breaks the act of writing the memo into stages and operations: analyzing the corpus, then using it to produce new text. 

Each is a *move*. The student articulates what it is, what it requires the system to know, and what counts as a good versus a bad version of it.

### 03 — run, read, fix

The first version of the memo will be flat in places, fluent over uncertainty in others, possibly wrong about Calvino in ways that are interesting. The student reads it the way Calvino would have read it: noticing where the cadence breaks, where the conceptual move was borrowed from a different writer entirely, where the model has confused multiplicity with mere accumulation. Then they change the corpus or the prompt chain, and run it again.

### 04 — defend it

The student submits the system, the final memo, and a written defense of both. 

This may also include an oral component. The oral defense is where the instructor confirms that the student, not the system, knows what was done and why.
